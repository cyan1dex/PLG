Disability Discrimination And Failure to Accommodate

California law protects disabled workers (qualified individuals with disabilities) from employment discrimination. An individual may be considered “disabled” if he or she has a physical or mental impairment that “in any way” limits one or more major life activities, by making the activity “difficult.” Major life activities most commonly include activities such as eating, sleeping, reproduction, walking, performing even a single job, hearing, speaking, breathing, caring for oneself, learning, interacting with others, and performing manual tasks. California law specifically enumerates certain conditions, such as diabetes, epilepsy, clinical depression, bipolar disorder, HIV and/or AIDS, heart disease, and hepatitis, as disabilities (regardless of severity). Under California law, employers have a duty to provide a qualified disabled employee with reasonable accommodation. If an employee requests and identifies a reasonable accommodation that would allow him or her to perform his or her duties, the employer is required to engage in a timely interactive process with the employee, to determine whether the employee may be reasonably accommodated at work. Such reasonable accommodations would include (1) alterations that make facilities readily accessible to the disabled, and (2) a restructuring of the job by reassignment, transfer, modification of equipment, or an alteration of duties.

If you believe that your employer has discriminated against you based on your disability by not acting in good faith during the reasonable accommodation process or by failing to propose a reasonable accommodation that allows you to perform the duties of your employment, you may have a claim for disability discrimination for failure to accommodate a disability and/or for failure to engage in the interactive process.
